# Chapter 3: Boathouse Showdown

The Boathouse loomed over the lake like a sleeping giant. By night, with the park emptied of joggers and tourists, it belonged to the birds. Specifically, it belonged to Razor.

Nutkin studied the structure from a willow tree forty yards out. Two crow sentries on the roof, one circling the east wall in a lazy patrol. Sloppy. Razor was confident. Confident was good — confident made mistakes.

The drainage grate on the lake side was barely wider than Nutkin's shoulders. He squeezed through sideways, fur scraping against rusted iron, and dropped into a service tunnel that ran beneath the building. The air was damp and smelled of algae and old popcorn.

He followed the tunnel upward through a gap in the floorboards and emerged behind a stack of overturned canoes. The interior of the Boathouse was a single vast space — rafters above, concrete below, and in the center, standing on a workbench under a shaft of moonlight: the golden acorn.

It was smaller than he remembered. Barely larger than his fist. But it gleamed with a weight that had nothing to do with gold.

And there was Razor.

The crow was enormous — twice the size of any bird Nutkin had faced. He sat on a rafter directly above the acorn, head tucked under one wing, apparently asleep. But Nutkin knew better. Crows didn't sleep. Not really.

Nutkin moved. Silent. Low. He crossed the floor in six seconds, reached the workbench, and closed his paw around the acorn.

"I was wondering when you'd show up."

Razor's voice came from above, calm and amused. The crow hadn't moved, but one yellow eye was open, gleaming in the dark.

"You SIS squirrels. Always so predictable."

Nutkin didn't wait for the monologue. He bolted — straight for the window. Razor screamed and dove, talons out. The two sentries crashed through the skylight, sending glass everywhere.

What followed was sixty seconds of pure chaos.

Nutkin ricocheted off a canoe, swung from a hanging rope, and launched himself through a broken window onto the roof. Razor was right behind, wings churning the air. A talon caught Nutkin's coat, ripping the sleeve clean off. Nutkin spun, pulled the tranquilizer pen, and fired.

The dart caught Razor in the neck. The big crow wobbled once, twice, and crashed onto the shingles, sliding to a stop at the gutter's edge.

The sentries were circling back. Nutkin didn't have time to admire his work. He leaped from the roof to the nearest tree, then the next, then the next — a blur of fur and shadow, the golden acorn clutched to his chest.

---

By the time the sun rose over Central Park, Nutkin was back in the hollow oak. Director Hazel took the acorn without ceremony, cracked it open with a specialized tool, and held the micro-chip up to the glow-worm light.

"Everything's intact," she said. "The caches are secure."

"And the Talon Circle?"

"Razor's in custody. His crew scattered. But…" She hesitated. "The leader — the one they call Corvus — we still don't have a name. Or a face."

Nutkin leaned against the wall of the briefing chamber. His coat was ruined. He had splinters in places he didn't want to think about. And he was starving.

"So it's not over," he said.

"It's never over, Nutkin."

He grabbed a hazelnut from the emergency stash by the door and cracked it with his teeth.

"Then I'll be ready."

Director Hazel almost smiled. Almost.

"Dismissed, Agent."

Nutkin climbed out of the oak and into the morning light. The park was waking up — dogs, runners, children. None of them had any idea what had happened in the dark. None of them ever would.

And somewhere out there, Corvus was watching.

But that was a problem for another day.
