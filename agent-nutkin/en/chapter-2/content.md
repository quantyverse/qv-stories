# Chapter 2: The Bird Feeder Rendezvous

The bird feeder hung from a rusted chain at the edge of the Ramble, tilted at an angle that said *nobody has filled this in years*. Its wooden roof was cracked, and the feeding tray was thick with old moss. To a human, it was junk. To the intelligence community of Central Park, it was Station Echo — one of the Talon Circle's oldest dead-drop points.

Nutkin approached from the elm side, moving branch to branch in complete silence. He'd coated his claws in pine resin to kill the sound. Old trick. Still worked.

The feeder was empty. But not untouched.

He dropped inside and ran his paw along the feeding tray. Scratch marks — deep, deliberate, made by something bigger than a sparrow. Crow claws. And there, wedged into a crack in the wood: a single black feather with a notch cut into the shaft. A Talon Circle route marker.

"They were here," Nutkin muttered. "But they moved it."

A flutter of wings above. Nutkin froze, one paw on the miniature tranquilizer pen strapped to his thigh. But it was just a pigeon — fat, gray, jittery. It landed on the rim of the feeder and immediately began bobbing its head in that way pigeons do when they're terrified.

"You Nutkin?" the pigeon whispered.

"Depends who's asking."

"Name's Coo. I, uh — I see things. Hear things. I got something for you, if you got something for me."

Nutkin produced a small foil packet from his coat. Breadcrumbs — the good kind, from the artisan bakery on 72nd Street. Coo's eyes went wide.

"Talk," Nutkin said.

"The crows moved something south last night. Small package, heavy guard. Three of 'em, maybe four. Headed for the Boathouse."

"The Boathouse? That's Razor's territory."

"Yeah." Coo swallowed nervously. "Razor's got it. The golden thing. He's keeping it there until a magpie courier shows up tomorrow at dawn."

Dawn. That gave Nutkin less than twelve hours.

"How many sentries?"

"I don't know, man. I don't go near the Boathouse. That place gives me the coos — I mean, the creeps."

Nutkin pocketed the breadcrumbs, leaving one on the tray. "You did good, Coo. Forget you ever saw me."

"Already forgotten," the pigeon said, and exploded upward in a clumsy burst of feathers.

Nutkin looked south, toward the dark shape of the Boathouse reflected in the lake. Razor. A crow with a reputation for cracking walnuts open with a single strike. The kind of bird who enjoyed his work.

This wasn't going to be subtle.

Nutkin pulled out his grappling hook — a paper clip bent into shape with dental floss for cord — and started moving.
